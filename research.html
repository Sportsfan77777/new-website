Making simulations of planet-induced vortices more realistic.

MNRAS paper (04/2017): Slowly-growing gap-opening planets trigger weaker vortices

ERES Conference presentation: ERES.pptx, PDF Version, (Conference website here)
Steward Internal Symposium presentation: Internal_Symposium.pptx, PDF Version

The Atacama Large Millimeter/Sub-mm Array (ALMA) has revolutionized the field of planet formation with its amazing capability to take good images of nearby protoplanetary disks! (Most of the closest ones are 140 to 160 pc away.) What would you expect these disks to look like? You might expect something like the figure below on the left: a disk that fades far from the star, that also has some rings and gaps, and that the gaps might contain planets. The feature you would probably take most for granted is that the disk is axisymmetric. However, not all disks we have imaged have that last, most obivous feature.

      

Left: AS 209, a normal axisymmetric disk highlighted by rings and gaps. Right: HD 135344B, an unusual asymmetric disk with a large crescent-shaped feature that stands out the most.

What is causing the crescent-shaped feature above on the right? How about a planet? As the movie below on the left shows, placing a Jupiter-mass planet in a smooth disk will create a gap in the disk. If the disk has sufficiently low viscosity, it will become unstable. The instability manifests as a series of small co-orbital vortices. In time, these vortices will merge into one big vortex that looks like the crescent in HD 135344B at the beginning. This vortex survives several thousand planet orbits, thereby providing a suitable explanation for that crescent (according to previous work).

      

Left: Rapidly-grown planet-induced vortex. Right: Slowly-grown planet-induced vortex.

But wait a minute: How could you ever have a fully-grown Jupiter-mass planet in a smooth disk with no gap? After reasoning that doesn't make sense if the planet formed through core accretion, we tried introducing the planet more slowly over a timescale of several hundred to a few thousand orbits (see the movie above on the right for an example). We found that the vortex is much weaker in two very noticeable ways! First, the lifetime is much shorter (by 80% in the two movies shown above!). Second, the vortex is much more elongated (with an azimuthal extent of 240 degrees, instead of 120 degrees with rapid growth!). In some cases with higher viscosity than shown in the movies above, the slower growth inhibits the formation of vortices altogether!

          

Left: Comparison of vortex lifetimes for different planet masses and disk viscosities. Right: Comparison of vortex extents. Yellow indicates extent ~120 degrees; black ~180 degrees; blue ~240 degrees. Red indicates no vortex!

For the public:

Condensed summary

I study how to find evidence of planets forming around other stars. We want to know how the planets in our solar system formed but we can’t go back in time to see them form. Our only option is to look for planets around other stars that are much younger than the Sun. Although these planets are too far away to see, we can see giant planet-forming disks around these stars. For my research, I run simulations of these disks to find ways of knowing if planets we can’t see are forming in disks around young stars.
